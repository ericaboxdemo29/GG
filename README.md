    <section>
        <h2>The Vision & Planning (1916–1932)</h2>
        <p>Before the bridge, the only practical way to travel between San Francisco and Marin County was by ferry. As the city grew, the bottleneck became restrictive, sparking calls for a bridge to span the treacherous Golden Gate Strait.</p>
        <ul>
            <li><strong>1916:</strong> Michael O'Shaughnessy, San Francisco's city engineer, began consulting engineers about feasibility. Most estimated costs up to $100 million and declared it impossible due to strong tides, deep waters, and heavy winds.</li>
            <li><strong>Joseph Strauss:</strong> An ambitious engineer, Strauss insisted he could build it safely and much cheaper (initially proposing a cantilever-suspension hybrid). He spent over a decade campaigning to win support from local governments, the military, and voters.</li>
            <li><strong>The Redesign:</strong> Under the influence of design champions Leon Moisseiff and Charles Alton Ellis, the ugly hybrid design was replaced with the sleek, graceful suspension design we recognize today. Irving Morrow contributed the Art Deco styling and chose the iconic "International Orange" color.</li>
        </ul>
    </section>

    <section>
        <h2>Construction (1933–1937)</h2>
        <p>Construction began on January 5, 1933, amidst the hardships of the Great Depression. The project provided thousands of vital jobs and was completed ahead of schedule and under budget.</p>
        <ul>
            <li><strong>The Safety Net:</strong> Chief Engineer Joseph Strauss prioritized safety, installing an innovative net below the construction site. It saved 19 lives (these survivors became known as the "Half Way to Hell Club"). Sadly, near completion, a scaffold fell, tearing the net and resulting in the deaths of 10 workers.</li>
            <li><strong>The Challenge:</strong> Workers battled freezing Pacific waters, thick San Francisco fog, blasting winds, and the difficulty of anchoring the south tower deep in the open ocean floor.</li>
        </ul>
    </section>

    <section>
        <h2>Historical Timeline</h2>
        <ul class="timeline">
            <li class="timeline-item"><span class="year">January 5, 1933:</span> Official start of construction.</li>
            <li class="timeline-item"><span class="year">May 27, 1937:</span> Opening Day. First opened to pedestrians (approx. 200,000 crossed), followed by vehicular traffic the next day.</li>
            <li class="timeline-item"><span class="year">1987:</span> 50th Anniversary. An estimated 300,000 pedestrians crowded the bridge, temporarily flattening the span under the immense weight.</li>
            <li class="timeline-item"><span class="year">1994:</span> Named one of the Wonders of the Modern World by the American Society of Civil Engineers.</li>
        </ul>
    </section>
